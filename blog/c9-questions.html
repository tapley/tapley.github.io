U3.W9: Questions

Apr. 26, 2015

Prompt: Analyze some of the questions you've asked (either in the community, to a a friend, or in Google). Were these good questions? What was good/ not so good about them? How could you make them better so you are more likely to get an answer?

I think one of the biggest mistakes people make when asking questions is trying to look smart. We often associate a person's questions with their intelligence â€” if it's too simple, that person must not be understanding the material at hand. Therefore, we can ask overly-nuanced questions that allow us to appear intelligent while offering little gain in knowledge.

The trick to asking good questions is to ask groups of questions, rather than singular ones. This allows the questioner to start small and then build a progression of logic. This will lead to a better understanding of the larger picture, and it will uncover any flawed assumptions that a more nuanced question would not have uncovered. By the end of a good progression of questions, the chain of logic may even progress to the type of nuanced questions to which most of us default.

Asking a series of questions therefore allows for two great outcomes: You can uncover a flawed assumption at simpler levels (and look smart!) or you can reach a point of nuanced discussion while understanding the entire subject at hand (and look smart!).
