Big O notation is a way of measuring the run-time of an algorithm. Using a limiting rule, one can simplify the comparisons between algorithms as the amount of data they use moves towards infinity. For example:

f(n) = n**2 + n + 5
g(n) = n**2

For small n, the presence of (n+5) will be significant when comparing f(n) and g(n). However, as n approaches infinity, (n+5) becomes insignificant. This idea leads to the following conclusion:

f(n) = O(n**2)
g(n) = O(n**2)

Thus, in the comparison of asymptotic run-times, f(n) can be considered as equal to g(n). A good way to think about Big-O notation is to remove everything but the highest-order term. In the example above, lower-order terms n and 5 were removed.

The formal (and therefore more confusing) mathematical definition is as follows:

f(x) = O(g(x)) iff |f(x)| <= C |g(x)| for all x > N.

For further information on Big O notation, check out courses by Stanford and Princeton on Coursera.
