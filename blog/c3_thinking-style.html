Fixing mindsets in education

Mar. 17, 2015

Part of our preparation for DBC has included learning about the work of Carol Dweck and her colleagues on the differences between fixed and growth mindsets. There’s a decent summary of the concept at Brainpickings (http://www.brainpickings.org/index.php/2014/01/29/carol-dweck-mindset/), but I’d also recommend watching Carol Dweck’s talk at the Melbourne Happiness and Its Causes conference (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QGvR_0mNpWM).

Gweck’s conclusion is that the dichotomy between fixed and growth mindset can permeate into nearly every region in our lives, and she gives countless examples across several different fields: Those who have a fixed mindset are almost always worse-off.

Unfortunately, the education that many of our brightest minds receive often rewards a fixed mindset, especially in the application process for colleges.

The college process is damaging because it discourages risk in students. Rather than trying to grow their minds by trying new things — and thereby risking failing — an aspiring college applicant would be far better served by choosing classes at which he or she can excel by simply proving the knowledge he or she already possesses.

Admissions officers may attest that they value risk-taking students, and that may be true. However, the chance of acceptance to a top school continually reaches new lows, and admissions officers themselves admit that their school turns away many qualified applicants. If a qualified applicant can be turned away, why would anyone risk having a low grade on their report card?

Unfortunately, I don’t have a solution to the college process. I went through high school with this fixed mindset, and it worked for me. I’m not sure I would have been accepted had I not strategically chosen classes that, while advanced, clearly played to my strengths, and I still believe it was worth it — I wouldn’t trade my time at Yale for anything.

So what should I have done differently?

My own issue with a fixed mindset was knowing when to stop. I spent far too much time playing to my own personal strengths in my classes at Yale — I knew I could write well, and I knew I could formulate arguments, so I chose a major that lent itself to essays and debates. I had a great experience with those classes, but I could’ve gained so much more from Yale had I approached my education with a risk-taking growth mindset.

The bad news is that I didn’t realize this until graduation. The good news is the experience of DBC embodies the risk, intellectual expansion, and deviance from the norm of a growth mindset, and I don’t think I would have been willing to undertake DBC if I wasn’t already transitioning into a growth mindset. The trick now is to just keep growing.
